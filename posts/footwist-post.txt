8:T766,<p>I mostly think of rigid body poses in terms of 4x4 transformation matrices. For
orientation, there are tools for visualization
(<a href="https://quaternions.online/">example</a>) and conversion between representations
(<a href="https://www.andre-gaschler.com/rotationconverter/">3D Rotation Converter</a>).</p>
<p>I was reading the robotics textbook by
<a href="https://www.cds.caltech.edu/~murray/books/MLS/">MLS</a> to learn more about
twists and screws. I made <a href="https://robowager.github.io/footwist/">footwist</a>
(<a href="https://github.com/robowager/footwist">repo</a>), a tool for visualization of
twists and screws. You can specify a pose in the form of translation and
orientation (as quaternion), and the tool calculates the equivalent twist and
screw. It ends up demonstrating <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chasles%27_theorem_(kinematics)">Chasles'
theorem</a>, which
states that any rigid body transform can be realized by a screw. You could also
use the GUI to update the twist or screw, and the other representations will be
calculated.</p>
<p>The project was a good opportunity to learn some JavaScript. The linter I used
(eslint with <a href="https://github.com/airbnb/javascript">airbnb</a> style) was a great
teacher.  The libraries I used were:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://mathjs.org/">mathjs</a> for all mapping and conversions, basically
implementing the math in the MLS book.</li>
<li><a href="https://threejs.org/">three</a> for very simple visualization. I could have
also used three for a lot of the matrix math.</li>
<li><a href="https://lil-gui.georgealways.com/">lil-gui</a> for the GUI, because that's what
a number of three examples used.</li>
</ul>
<p>Visualizing the screw that achieves a transform helps with intuition, but it's
also fun to just play around with values.</p>
